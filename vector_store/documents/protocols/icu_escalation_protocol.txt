ICU Escalation Protocol

Purpose

This simulated protocol establishes standardized criteria for recognizing clinical deterioration requiring escalation to intensive care evaluation within the MIRA demonstration system. The protocol supports Retrieval-Augmented Generation benchmarking and is not intended for actual clinical use.

Scope

This protocol applies to adult inpatients experiencing acute clinical deterioration, worsening respiratory status, cardiovascular instability, neurological decline, severe infection, or other conditions requiring rapid reassessment.

Recognition of Clinical Deterioration

Potential indicators include:

• Persistent hypotension

• Rapid heart rate

• Low oxygen saturation

• Increased respiratory distress

• Altered mental status

• Decreased urine output

• Severe laboratory abnormalities

Clinical deterioration should prompt immediate reassessment.

Respiratory Criteria

Escalation should be considered for patients with:

• Increasing oxygen requirements

• Respiratory rate significantly above baseline

• Severe shortness of breath

• Cyanosis

• Inability to maintain adequate oxygenation

Patients with COPD or heart failure should receive careful evaluation because respiratory symptoms may worsen rapidly.

Cardiovascular Criteria

Escalation should be considered for:

• Persistent hypotension

• Chest pain

• New arrhythmias

• Poor perfusion

• Syncope

Patients with heart failure should receive additional evaluation for worsening volume overload or cardiogenic instability.

Neurological Criteria

Urgent evaluation is recommended for:

• Sudden confusion

• Reduced level of consciousness

• New focal neurological deficits

• Seizure activity

• Suspected stroke

Neurological deterioration should never be ignored.

Sepsis Considerations

Patients with suspected infection who demonstrate hypotension, altered mental status, elevated lactate, persistent fever, or rapidly worsening clinical condition should receive prompt reassessment and possible ICU consultation.

Laboratory Review

Critical laboratory abnormalities should trigger provider notification.

Examples include:

• Severe electrolyte abnormalities

• Markedly abnormal blood glucose

• Rising creatinine

• Critical hemoglobin values

• Elevated lactate

Clinical judgment should always accompany laboratory interpretation.

Escalation Actions

Escalation may include:

• Rapid Response Team activation

• ICU consultation

• Senior physician notification

• Repeat laboratory testing

• Additional monitoring

• Immediate bedside evaluation

Escalation should occur promptly whenever patient safety is at risk.

Documentation Requirements

Documentation should include:

• Reason for escalation

• Clinical findings

• Vital signs

• Laboratory abnormalities

• Provider notification

• Response time

• Outcome of escalation

Quality Metrics

Suggested quality indicators include:

• Time from deterioration recognition to provider notification

• ICU transfer documentation completeness

• Rapid Response Team activation time

• Documentation completeness

• Unexpected ICU transfer rate
