ADA Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes — Clinical Summary

OVERVIEW AND CLASSIFICATION

Diabetes mellitus is classified into several categories: type 1 diabetes, resulting from autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells and absolute insulin deficiency; type 2 diabetes, characterized by progressive loss of insulin secretion combined with insulin resistance; gestational diabetes, diagnosed during pregnancy; and other specific types including monogenic diabetes syndromes and drug- or chemical-induced diabetes. Type 2 diabetes accounts for the large majority of cases and is the primary focus of population-level screening and management programs in most health systems.

Diagnostic criteria for diabetes include any of the following: a fasting plasma glucose of 126 mg/dL or greater, a 2-hour plasma glucose of 200 mg/dL or greater during a 75-gram oral glucose tolerance test, an A1c of 6.5% or greater, or a random plasma glucose of 200 mg/dL or greater in a patient with classic symptoms of hyperglycemia. Prediabetes is defined by a fasting glucose between 100 and 125 mg/dL, a 2-hour OGTT value between 140 and 199 mg/dL, or an A1c between 5.7% and 6.4%.

GLYCEMIC TARGETS

The general A1c target for most non-pregnant adults with diabetes is less than 7%. This target reflects a balance between reducing the risk of microvascular complications and avoiding the harms of intensive treatment, particularly hypoglycemia. A more stringent target, below 6.5%, may be appropriate for selected patients with short disease duration, long life expectancy, and no significant cardiovascular disease, provided this can be achieved without significant hypoglycemia or excessive treatment burden. Conversely, a less stringent target of below 8% is appropriate for patients with a history of severe hypoglycemia, limited life expectancy, advanced microvascular or macrovascular complications, extensive comorbid conditions, or long-standing diabetes in whom the general target is difficult to achieve despite intensive self-management education, appropriate glucose monitoring, and effective doses of multiple glucose-lowering agents.

Glycemic targets should be individualized based on patient-specific factors rather than applied uniformly. Factors that argue for tighter control include newly diagnosed disease, long remaining life expectancy, absence of significant cardiovascular disease, and patient motivation and capacity for self-management. Factors that argue for less stringent control include advanced age, limited life expectancy, presence of significant comorbidities, established vascular complications, and a history of hypoglycemia unawareness.

TARGETS FOR OLDER ADULTS

For adults aged 65 and older, glycemic targets should account for functional status, cognitive status, and life expectancy rather than chronological age alone. Healthy older adults with few coexisting chronic conditions and intact cognitive and functional status should have glycemic and blood pressure targets similar to those of younger adults, generally an A1c below 7.0% to 7.5%. Older adults with multiple coexisting chronic illnesses, mild to moderate cognitive impairment, or two or more instrumental activities of daily living impairments should have a somewhat less stringent target, generally an A1c below 8.0%. Older adults with complex or poor health status, including those with long-term care needs, moderate to severe cognitive impairment, or two or more activities of daily living dependencies, should have the least stringent target, generally an A1c below 8.5%, with a primary goal of avoiding hypoglycemia and symptomatic hyperglycemia rather than tight control. In this population, simplification of complex regimens is often prioritized over strict numeric targets, and deprescribing of medications associated with a high risk of hypoglycemia, such as sulfonylureas or insulin, should be actively considered when appropriate.

SCREENING RECOMMENDATIONS

Screening for type 2 diabetes and prediabetes is recommended for all adults beginning at age 35, regardless of weight, with repeat screening at a minimum of every three years if results are normal. Screening should begin earlier, and be considered at any age, in adults who are overweight or obese and who have one or more additional risk factors, including a first-degree relative with diabetes, high-risk race or ethnicity, history of cardiovascular disease, hypertension, HDL cholesterol below 35 mg/dL or triglycerides above 250 mg/dL, polycystic ovary syndrome, physical inactivity, or other clinical conditions associated with insulin resistance such as severe obesity or acanthosis nigricans. Women diagnosed with gestational diabetes should have lifelong screening at least every three years thereafter, given their substantially elevated lifetime risk of developing type 2 diabetes.

PHARMACOLOGIC MANAGEMENT

Metformin remains the preferred initial pharmacologic agent for the treatment of type 2 diabetes in most patients, owing to its efficacy, favorable safety profile, low cost, and possible cardiovascular benefit. For patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, indicators of high cardiovascular risk, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease, the choice of additional glucose-lowering therapy should be influenced by these comorbidities independent of A1c level. In such patients, GLP-1 receptor agonists or SGLT2 inhibitors with demonstrated cardiovascular benefit are recommended as part of the regimen. For patients with heart failure or chronic kidney disease specifically, SGLT2 inhibitors with evidence of benefit in these conditions are preferred, given their demonstrated ability to reduce hospitalization for heart failure and slow the progression of kidney disease independent of glycemic effect.

Insulin therapy should be considered at any stage of type 2 diabetes when glycemic targets are not met with oral or non-insulin injectable agents, and is required in patients presenting with severe hyperglycemia, catabolic features such as unintentional weight loss, or ketosis. In type 1 diabetes, insulin therapy is required from diagnosis, typically delivered via multiple daily injections or continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion, with dosing individualized based on carbohydrate intake, activity level, and glucose monitoring data.

CHRONIC KIDNEY DISEASE CONSIDERATIONS

Diabetes is the leading cause of chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal disease in most developed health systems. Annual screening for kidney disease in patients with diabetes should include a urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio and an estimated glomerular filtration rate. Patients with diabetes and confirmed albuminuria, or those with reduced eGFR, should generally receive both an ACE inhibitor or angiotensin receptor blocker and an SGLT2 inhibitor, as these agent classes have each independently demonstrated the ability to slow progression of kidney disease. Metformin dosing requires adjustment or discontinuation as eGFR declines, generally requiring dose reduction below an eGFR of 45 and discontinuation below an eGFR of 30, due to the risk of lactic acidosis in the setting of reduced renal clearance.

CARDIOVASCULAR RISK MANAGEMENT

Because cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death among people with diabetes, comprehensive management extends well beyond glycemic control. Blood pressure targets for most adults with diabetes and hypertension are below 130/80 mmHg, individualized based on cardiovascular risk and treatment tolerance. Statin therapy is recommended for the majority of adults with diabetes, with intensity of therapy determined by age, presence of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, and additional risk factors. Antiplatelet therapy with aspirin is generally reserved for secondary prevention in patients with established cardiovascular disease rather than routine primary prevention, reflecting a shift in guidance based on trial evidence showing bleeding risk may offset benefit in lower-risk primary prevention populations.

HYPOGLYCEMIA MANAGEMENT

Hypoglycemia is defined as a blood glucose below 70 mg/dL, with clinically significant hypoglycemia defined as below 54 mg/dL. Patients treated with insulin or insulin secretagogues such as sulfonylureas should be assessed at every visit for hypoglycemia risk and history of hypoglycemic events. Recurrent severe hypoglycemia should prompt reassessment of the treatment regimen, including simplification of therapy, adjustment of glycemic targets, and consideration of continuous glucose monitoring, which has been shown to reduce hypoglycemic events compared to fingerstick monitoring alone.
