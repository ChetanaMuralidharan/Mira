ACC/AHA Heart Failure Guideline — Clinical Summary

CLASSIFICATION AND STAGING

Heart failure is classified along two complementary frameworks. The New York Heart Association functional classification describes symptom severity: Class I indicates no limitation of physical activity; Class II indicates slight limitation, with ordinary activity causing symptoms; Class III indicates marked limitation, with less-than-ordinary activity causing symptoms; and Class IV indicates symptoms at rest or with any physical activity. The ACC/AHA staging system describes disease progression: Stage A includes patients at risk for heart failure without structural heart disease or symptoms, such as those with hypertension, diabetes, or a family history of cardiomyopathy; Stage B includes patients with structural heart disease, such as reduced ejection fraction or prior myocardial infarction, but without current or prior symptoms; Stage C includes patients with structural heart disease and current or prior symptoms of heart failure; and Stage D includes patients with refractory heart failure requiring specialized interventions such as mechanical circulatory support, transplantation, or palliative care.

Heart failure is further categorized by ejection fraction. Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction refers to an ejection fraction of 40% or less. Heart failure with mildly reduced ejection fraction refers to an ejection fraction between 41% and 49%. Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction refers to an ejection fraction of 50% or greater, with symptoms attributable to diastolic dysfunction, elevated filling pressures, or other structural abnormalities. This distinction matters significantly for treatment selection, as many of the pharmacologic therapies with the strongest mortality benefit are proven specifically in the reduced ejection fraction population.

GUIDELINE-DIRECTED MEDICAL THERAPY FOR HFrEF

For patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, guideline-directed medical therapy comprises four pillars, each with independent mortality benefit demonstrated in randomized trials. The first pillar is an angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor, or alternatively an ACE inhibitor or angiotensin receptor blocker if the neprilysin inhibitor combination is not tolerated or available. The second pillar is a beta blocker with proven mortality benefit in heart failure, specifically carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, or bisoprolol, initiated at low dose and titrated upward as tolerated. The third pillar is a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, such as spironolactone or eplerenone, which requires monitoring of potassium and renal function given the risk of hyperkalemia. The fourth pillar is an SGLT2 inhibitor, which has demonstrated mortality and hospitalization benefit in heart failure independent of diabetes status, representing a major addition to standard therapy in recent years.

Current guidance emphasizes rapid initiation and simultaneous titration of all four pillar therapies where possible, rather than the historical sequential approach of adding one class at a time over months. Delaying initiation of proven therapies in favor of sequential titration has been associated with worse outcomes, since patients may experience clinical events during the delay period before reaching target doses.

DEVICE THERAPY

Patients with an ejection fraction of 35% or less who remain symptomatic despite optimized guideline-directed medical therapy for at least three months, and who have a reasonable expectation of survival with good functional status for more than one year, should be evaluated for implantable cardioverter-defibrillator placement for primary prevention of sudden cardiac death. Patients with a wide QRS complex, typically 150 milliseconds or greater with left bundle branch block morphology, and an ejection fraction of 35% or less despite optimal medical therapy, should be considered for cardiac resynchronization therapy, which can improve symptoms, reduce hospitalizations, and improve survival by correcting ventricular dyssynchrony.

MANAGEMENT OF HFPEF

Management of heart failure with preserved ejection fraction focuses on control of comorbidities including hypertension, atrial fibrillation, obesity, and coronary artery disease, alongside diuretic therapy for volume management. SGLT2 inhibitors have demonstrated benefit in reducing heart failure hospitalization in this population as well, representing one of the few pharmacologic classes with robust trial evidence across the full spectrum of ejection fraction. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists may provide modest benefit, particularly in patients with ejection fraction toward the lower end of the preserved range. Aggressive volume management with diuretics remains a mainstay of symptomatic treatment, though care must be taken to avoid overdiuresis given the sensitivity of this population to preload reduction.

ACUTE DECOMPENSATED HEART FAILURE AND READMISSION RISK

Acute decompensated heart failure is a leading cause of hospitalization, and readmission within 30 days of discharge is a key quality metric tracked by health systems and reimbursement programs. Risk factors for readmission include incomplete decongestion at discharge, inadequate titration of guideline-directed medical therapy prior to discharge, poor medication adherence, dietary sodium indiscretion, inadequate outpatient follow-up, and psychosocial factors including limited health literacy and lack of social support.

Discharge planning for heart failure admissions should include confirmation of euvolemia or a clear plan for outpatient diuresis, initiation or up-titration of guideline-directed medical therapy prior to discharge where hemodynamically tolerated, patient education on daily weight monitoring and sodium restriction, medication reconciliation to identify and address barriers to adherence, and a scheduled follow-up visit within 7 to 14 days of discharge. Early follow-up, ideally within one week, has been associated with meaningfully reduced 30-day readmission rates in multiple health system quality improvement studies.

MONITORING AND FOLLOW-UP

Patients with heart failure should have periodic reassessment of ejection fraction, particularly after initiation of guideline-directed medical therapy, since a meaningful proportion of patients with initially reduced ejection fraction show significant recovery with sustained optimal therapy. Natriuretic peptide levels, including BNP and NT-proBNP, are useful both for diagnostic evaluation of dyspnea and for monitoring treatment response, though absolute values must be interpreted in the context of renal function, body mass index, and atrial fibrillation status, all of which can affect these values independent of heart failure severity.
